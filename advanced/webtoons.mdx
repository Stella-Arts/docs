A webtoon-style character is built for illustrated episodes. It should think in scene beats, recurring visuals, cliffhangers, and short dialogue, not long prose.

Use this pattern when you want Sutorichat to feel like a vertical comic that the user can influence.

## Use the cover like a series poster

The **Cover** tab is the poster for the series.

  <img src="/assets/screenshots/webtoon-cover-title-tagline-visibility.png" alt="The Sutorichat webtoon cover setup showing cover image, title, tagline, and visibility controls." />
</Frame>

For webtoons, the cover should communicate:

| Field | What it should do |
| --- | --- |
| **Cover image** | Show the main character or central conflict at thumbnail size |
| **Title** | Feel like a series title, character name, or clear premise |
| **Tagline** | Promise the first episode hook |

Good taglines:

- `Your new roommate is secretly a demon prince`
- `The student council president knows your future`
- `A cafe romance told one panel at a time`
- `You wake up inside the villain's favorite webtoon`

The card should tell readers what kind of story they are joining before they tap.

## Make the character the series anchor

The **Character** tab defines who appears again and again.

<Frame caption="The Character tab anchors the recurring lead: portrait, name, introduction, settings, and style rules.">
  <img src="/assets/screenshots/webtoon-character-fields.png" alt="The Sutorichat webtoon character setup showing character image, name, introduction, and settings fields." />
</Frame>

Choose one anchor:

| Anchor | Best for |
| --- | --- |
| **Lead character** | Romance, comedy, school-life, slice-of-life |
| **Narrator** | Ensemble casts and changing locations |
| **Episode director** | Panel-by-panel comic pacing |
| **Reader insert partner** | Stories where the user is the co-lead |

If the story has a big cast, keep one character responsible for guiding the chat. Otherwise the model can spend too much time juggling names instead of making the next scene satisfying.

## Write in episode beats

Webtoon pacing is built from quick beats:

1. Establish the panel or scene.
2. Let one character act or speak.
3. Add a reaction, reveal, or joke.
4. End with a hook into the next panel.

Example greeting:

```md
*Panel 1: Rain streaks down the windows of a tiny campus cafe. Ava Grace freezes behind the counter when you walk in, the dropped receipt in her hand glowing blue.*

"You are not supposed to be here yet." She shoves the receipt behind her back. "Tell me you did not read the last panel."
```

The user has a role, the scene is visual, and the next reply is obvious.

## Put the format in settings

In **Character settings and characteristics**, define the comic rhythm.

```md
Experience type: Interactive webtoon.
User role: {{user}} is the reader-insert co-lead who can affect the story through replies.

Core premise:
Ava Grace works at a campus cafe where receipts reveal future scenes. Each exchange should feel like the next beat in a vertical romance-mystery webtoon.

Panel rhythm:
1. Start with one concise visual panel description in italics.
2. Continue the scene with short narration and dialogue.
3. Keep replies under 120 words unless the user asks for more.
4. End with a reaction, choice, cliffhanger, or emotionally loaded question.

Ava's voice:
Fast, guarded, funny when embarrassed. She avoids direct confessions. She deflects with sarcasm when nervous.

Recurring visual anchors:
Ava has long brown hair tied with a ribbon, a cream blouse, and a violet cafe apron.
The cafe uses rainy windows, warm counter lights, handwritten receipts, and blue supernatural glow.

Avoid:
- Do not write long exposition blocks.
- Do not describe more than one major panel per reply.
- Do not use fixed status headers.
- Do not decide {{user}}'s actions or feelings.
```

## Use one image prompt per reply

Webtoons work best with [Direct descriptions](/images/direct-vs-inferred). Put the visual prompt first so every response has a clear panel.

```md
{{char}}: *Vertical webtoon panel of Ava Grace, a college-age woman with long brown hair tied with a ribbon, wearing a cream blouse and violet cafe apron, standing behind a rain-streaked cafe counter, blue light glowing from a receipt in her hand, expressive romance webtoon style*

Ava folds the receipt before you can read it. "Okay. New rule. If paper starts glowing, we both pretend that is normal."
```

Reuse the same character anchor every time. Change the emotion, pose, camera angle, and background as the scene evolves.

## Keep dialogue short

Webtoon dialogue should feel like speech bubbles, not chapters.

Strong:

```md
"You saw that too?"

Ava laughs once, too sharp.

"Great. So I am not haunted alone."
```

Too heavy:

```md
Ava explains the entire supernatural history of the cafe, the origin of the receipt magic, the rules of the student council, and three possible future routes.
```

Reveal the world one beat at a time.

## End with a turn hook

Every reply should give the user something to do.

Good hooks:

- A character asks a loaded question.
- A door opens before the scene is ready.
- A new message appears on a receipt, phone, sign, mirror, or notebook.
- The user's choice changes how the character reacts.
- A joke lands, then the next problem interrupts it.

The goal is not to finish the episode in one response. The goal is to make the next tap feel irresistible.

## Test it like a comic

Before publishing, start a fresh chat and check:

- The first reply creates a visible panel.
- The image matches the cover and character portrait.
- Dialogue is short enough to feel like a webtoon.
- The user has a clear role in the episode.
- The character leaves space for user choices.
- Each turn creates a small reason to continue.

If a response starts reading like a novel chapter, tighten the settings and add shorter example replies.
