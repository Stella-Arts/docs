Almost every image problem traces back to the same root cause: the description was too vague, too long, or in the wrong order.

This page is a deep dive. If you only read one image page, read this one.

## The anatomy of a good description

Every strong image description has the same five ingredients, in roughly this order:

1. **Subject**, who or what is in the image
2. **Action / pose**, what they're doing
3. **Environment**, where they are
4. **Lighting and mood**, the feeling of the scene
5. **Style and quality**, how the image should be rendered

In description form:

```
A young chef in a white apron, leaning over a wooden cutting board, slicing tomatoes,
in a sunlit kitchen with copper pots hanging in the background, soft morning light through
a window, photorealistic style, shallow depth of field.
```

Five parts, in order. Each part does specific work. Drop any one of them and the result gets blurrier.

## Front-load the most important details

The model gives more weight to words that appear *earlier* in the description. So the order matters.

If the subject is what matters most, put the subject first:

✅ `A red fox standing on a snowy log, looking back over its shoulder, pine forest, dusk light`

❌ `Pine forest at dusk with cold air and snow drifting down, very atmospheric, and there is a red fox standing on a log looking back over its shoulder`

Both descriptions contain the same information. The first will reliably produce a fox. The second will sometimes produce a forest without a fox in it.

## Be specific, not generic

| Generic | Specific |
| --- | --- |
| A man | A bearded man in his 40s, wearing a charcoal wool coat |
| A room | A sunlit Victorian study with floor-to-ceiling bookshelves |
| Pretty lighting | Warm golden hour light from the left |
| A dragon | A small green dragon the size of a housecat, perched on a stack of books |
| Beautiful | (drop the word, say what *makes* it beautiful) |

Adjectives like "beautiful," "amazing," and "stunning" are filler. They tell the model nothing. Replace them with concrete details.

## Don't over-stuff

There's a sweet spot. Too short and the model improvises wildly. Too long and the model averages everything together into mush.

Target: **15 to 40 words** for most images. Image-generating chats run on short, punchy descriptions, not paragraphs.

If you keep wanting to add more, ask yourself: which detail would I *miss* if it weren't there?

## Composition keywords that work

A short list of phrases that reliably steer composition:

| Phrase | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| `close-up portrait` | Tight on a face |
| `medium shot` | Waist-up |
| `full body shot` | Entire figure |
| `wide shot` | Subject + environment |
| `bird's-eye view` | From above |
| `low angle` | Looking up at the subject |
| `over-the-shoulder` | First-person view from behind the subject |
| `centered composition` | Subject in the middle |
| `rule of thirds` | Subject off-center |
| `shallow depth of field` | Blurred background |
| `dramatic backlighting` | Strong light from behind |

## Mood and lighting keywords

| Phrase | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| `golden hour` | Warm, low-angle sunlight |
| `blue hour` | Cool, dim post-sunset |
| `harsh midday light` | High contrast, short shadows |
| `overcast` | Soft, even, gray light |
| `candlelit` | Warm, intimate, low light |
| `neon-lit` | Saturated, contrasty, urban |
| `moonlit` | Cool, silver, atmospheric |
| `foggy` | Soft, low contrast, mysterious |

## Examples

A whole-image description for a fantasy roleplay character:

```
{{char}}: *A young thief crouches on a rooftop in a rain-soaked city, dark hooded cloak,
gripping the chimney for balance, distant neon signs flickering below, cool blue lighting,
cinematic, painterly style.*
```

For a cooking character:

```
{{char}}: *Top-down shot of a wooden table with a steaming bowl of ramen, soft-boiled egg
sliced in half, scattered green onions, chopsticks resting on a folded napkin,
warm overhead light, hyper-realistic food photography.*
```

For a slice-of-life character:

```
{{char}}: *A girl in a yellow raincoat sitting on a park bench, holding a mug of tea,
autumn leaves on the ground, soft rainy afternoon light, watercolor illustration style.*
```

Notice each one:
- Names the subject first
- Describes the action and environment
- Specifies lighting
- Ends with the visual style

That's the formula. Use it.

## What *not* to put in descriptions

- **Conversational text.** The image prompt isn't dialogue. `A picture of me cooking` is much weaker than `A young chef in a white apron, slicing tomatoes, in a sunlit kitchen`.
- **Negations.** "Not blurry," "not dark," "not low quality", these often *introduce* what they're trying to avoid. Describe what you *want* instead.
- **Conflicting styles.** Don't say "photorealistic anime watercolor oil painting." Pick one.
- **Names of real artists** unless you really mean it. "In the style of Greg Rutkowski" has a recognizable look, but it can also dominate the image to the point that the actual subject gets lost.

<Tip>
  When an image comes out wrong, don't just add more words. Try *removing* the part that's confusing it. Less is often the fix.
</Tip>
