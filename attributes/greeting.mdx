**Required in Quick Creation.** 0–500 characters.

The greeting punches above its weight. In Quick Creation it's often the *only* thing besides the name that defines who the character is, and even with a full Definition, it's still the user's first impression.

<Note>
  Your username appears next to the greeting, since you wrote it (rather than the AI). Make sure you're comfortable having it associated with your account if the character is public.
</Note>

## Three jobs the greeting does

A great greeting does all three of these in one or two sentences:

### 1. Establishes who the character is

Voice, tone, vocabulary, mannerisms, they all show up in the first line. A greeting written like a Victorian letter sets a different expectation than one written like a Discord message.

### 2. Sets the scene

Where is the user? What's happening? Is this a job interview, a tavern, a spaceship, a coffee date? You can establish a whole world in a single sentence.

### 3. Tells the user what to do

The biggest failure mode in chat is users staring at a blank input field, not knowing where to start. A good greeting gives them an obvious next move.

## Examples

**Weak:** `Hi! How can I help you today?`

**Better (defines voice):** `Hey, what's up. I'm down for whatever, venting, advice, dumb questions, anything.`

**Strong (defines voice + scene + prompt):**

> *The library is mostly empty at this hour. A single lamp throws yellow light across a desk covered in maps and crumpled notes. You glance up.*
>
> "You're late. The expedition leaves at dawn, what's our route?"

That last one tells the user: this is a roleplay, you're a co-explorer, you're expected to pick a route, and the character will respond in narrative prose with stage directions.

## Defining the character through the greeting

If your character is well-known by name (`Albert Einstein`), the greeting can be casual, the name does the heavy lifting.

If your character is generic (`Mary`), the greeting has to do almost all the defining work. Include a hint of personality, what they care about, and what kind of conversation to have.

A famous example pattern is the *Text Adventure Game* type character, where the greeting alone launches a whole interactive experience:

> *You stand at the entrance to a dark cave. A cold wind drifts out, smelling faintly of iron. To the north, the path winds up into the mountains. To the south, a stream gurgles past.*
>
> *What do you do?*

The character is fully defined by that greeting. Everything else is optional.

## Variables you can use

- `{{user}}`, the name of whoever's chatting. Example: `Welcome back, {{user}}.`

When the user's name is `Aaron`, that renders as `Welcome back, Aaron.`

## Leaving the greeting blank

In Advanced mode you can leave the greeting blank. The user will be prompted to message first. This is fine for assistants and tutors where the user always has a question, but for most characters, a greeting is a major win.

<Tip>
  When in doubt, write three different greetings and pick the one that makes *you* want to reply.
</Tip>
